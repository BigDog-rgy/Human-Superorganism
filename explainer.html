Part I

The Hebbian Brain

By the late 1940s, neurophysiology and psychology had reached an awkward impasse. Adrian and Sherrington had established the basic mechanics of the neuron — the all-or-nothing firing, the synaptic junction, the refractory period. Psychology, meanwhile, had accumulated a substantial body of observations about learning, perception, and memory. The two literatures sat beside each other without touching. There was no mechanistic account of how neural activity could produce organized behavior, and no serious attempt to build one.

Donald Hebb's The Organization of Behavior (1949) was that attempt. His starting premise was stringent: if learning is real, it must be physically instantiated somewhere in the nervous system, and the most plausible candidate was the synapse. He proposed that when the axon of one cell repeatedly contributes to the firing of another, some metabolic or structural change occurs at the junction that increases the efficiency of the connection. This is the postulate now condensed into the phrase neurons that fire together, wire together — though the economy of that formulation flattens what Hebb was actually doing, which was deriving a neurophysiological mechanism capable of explaining the full range of cognitive phenomena from first principles.

The central structural consequence of the synaptic rule is what Hebb called the cell assembly: a distributed, reverberant circuit of cortical cells that, through repeated co-activation, comes to function as a unit. The assembly is not anatomically fixed — it is a learned configuration, grown from experience. Crucially, it exhibits a property that the individual synapse does not: it can sustain its own activity briefly after the initiating stimulus is withdrawn. A partial cue is sufficient to reactivate the whole. This reverberatory dynamic was Hebb's account of how a fleeting stimulus leaves a persisting trace — the neural substrate of short-term memory and the persistence of thought.

A single assembly, however, cannot account for directed or sequential cognition. Hebb proposed that assemblies excite one another in learned succession — each activation priming the conditions for the next — forming what he called a phase sequence. Thought, on this model, is not a static state but a trajectory: a temporally extended cascade of assembly activations moving through channels carved by prior experience. The richness of cognition falls out not from the complexity of any individual cell's behavior, but from the organization of the connections between them.

Part II

Superorganisms

The biologist William Morton Wheeler coined the term superorganism in 1911 to describe something he couldn't stop noticing in ant colonies: the colony behaves as if it has intentions. It hunts, allocates labor, manages temperature, wages war, and tends its young — all without any individual ant understanding the whole. Wheeler's intuition was that the colony was the real organism, and the individual ant was something more like a cell.

The concept matured through the twentieth century as researchers documented that eusocial insects — ants, bees, wasps, termites — exhibit a level of coordinated complexity that strains purely individualist explanations. E.O. Wilson and Bert Hölldobler's The Superorganism (2009) gave the framework its most complete treatment: the colony as a unit of natural selection, with castes playing roles analogous to organ systems, and chemical signaling serving as a kind of distributed nervous system. No individual carries the plan. The plan is the colony.

The examples extend beyond insects. The Portuguese man o' war is not a single animal — it is a consortium of physically distinct polyps, each specialized for locomotion, predation, digestion, or reproduction. The slime mold Physarum polycephalum is perhaps the most striking: thousands of independent amoebae that, under stress, aggregate into a single coordinated body capable of navigating mazes and solving network optimization problems. Collective intelligence without a brain.

Once you have that concept in hand, certain questions become hard to avoid. If an ant colony achieves goal-directed, adaptive behavior through the interaction of simple agents following local rules — what prevents us from saying something similar about other large-scale systems? Markets. States. Ideological movements. The philosopher Eric Schwitzgebel pressed this question to its uncomfortable limit in If Materialism Is True, the United States Is Probably Conscious (2015): if consciousness is a functional property of systems that process information in the right way, and if a materialist account commits us to granting it wherever those conditions are met, then the inference to national-scale consciousness is hard to block without special pleading. Schwitzgebel isn't really arguing the US is conscious. He's arguing that our concepts are not as clean as we pretend — that genuine philosophical work remains at the boundary between individual and collective mind.

Part III

Introducing a Hebbian View of the Human Superorganism

Schwitzgebel's provocation rests on a specific claim: that consciousness, if it is anything at all, is a property of organized complexity rather than of substrate. The United States processes information, responds to its environment, maintains internal states, and acts in the world. If we want to deny it any form of inner life, we owe a principled account of what exactly disqualifies it — one that does not quietly rely on biological chauvinism.

The uncomfortable implication is not that nations are sentient in any warm, experiential sense. It is that organizational logic generating higher-order behavior may be real and consequential entirely independent of whether any unit within the system is aware of it. The ant colony does not know it is an organism. The neurons composing a cell assembly do not know they are encoding a concept. The gestalt activity is occurring at a level above the reach of any participant's awareness — and it is doing real work regardless.

Prime Mover Tracker is a vibe-coded attempt to take that seriously as an analytical frame. The living network of actors, coalitions, and strategic dynamics that shapes politics, technology, and capital is treated here as a system exhibiting exactly this kind of organized, higher-order behavior. Individual actors are mapped as neurons. Coalitions that habitually co-activate around a shared domain or strategic posture are cell assemblies. The higher-order dynamics that sequence which coalitions are active at any given moment are phase sequences. The assembled model, updated weekly from real-world evidence, is the superorganism.

The project is not making a claim about consciousness. It is borrowing the structural vocabulary Hebb developed for the brain because that vocabulary — built to describe distributed, learned, temporally organized behavior — appears to generalize.

Part IV

How It Was Built

The project runs on two tracks: a slow structural track for building and refreshing the underlying model, and a weekly operational track for generating briefings and updating learned state.

Candidate ingestion starts with structured public lists — Forbes billionaires, congressional membership, heads of state — and then expands through LLM-assisted councils that surface harder-to-source figures: technology executives, civil society leaders, actors whose influence crosses domains without being captured by any single ranking. These feeds are compiled into scope-specific master lists for the US and global models.

The ranking pipeline reduces that master list through three stages. A tournament filter runs groups of six candidates through LLM comparisons, keeping the top four, with a source-aware design: candidates are first grouped by source domain so that a religious leader or NGO director is not immediately forced to compete against five billionaires. Survivors then enter a Swiss-style ELO ranking in head-to-head matchups. A final boundary ELO pass re-ranks the cutoff window — the top 150 for the US model, top 300 globally — where inclusion noise matters most. None of the rankings are hand-maintained; the roster is produced end-to-end by the reduction pipeline.

The structural vocabulary — cell assemblies and phase sequences — is generated through a separate LLM council process. The CA canon is built neuron-centric: candidate assemblies are proposed, deduplicated, and narrowed by ELO. Phase sequences are then assigned CAs through a multi-model council and a chairman synthesis step. The assembled canons determine the network topology that all downstream steps inherit.

The weekly firing model is where the system most explicitly enacts the Hebbian analogy. Each run begins by fetching phase-sequence–level news via Perplexity and scoring each PS's activity from 1 to 10. The top-ranked PSes then fire cell assemblies through a coactivation-guided sequence: a seed CA is chosen at random, and each subsequent CA is drawn probabilistically, weighted by its learned coactivation score against the previously fired CA. The sequence is therefore not arbitrary but shaped by accumulated history of which assemblies have been observed as reinforcing or adversarial. Neurons are then selected conscious PS by PS: each actor's selection weight combines a base score, a bonus for CA membership in fired assemblies, and a further bonus from their neuron-neuron coactivation score against already-selected co-members of the same PS. A separate spontaneous layer — using a skip-counter probability that rises roughly 10% per missed week — ensures that dormant actors periodically resurface regardless of current PS activity.

Learning closes the loop. After each briefing, the coactivation updater runs every pair of co-active conscious neurons — and every pair of co-active CAs — through a lightweight LLM judgment: are these two entities reinforcing, adversarial, or neutral within their shared phase sequence this week? Scores are updated as a rolling weighted sum and decay 15% per week toward zero, so the memory is recency-biased rather than permanent. These scores feed directly back into the next week's selection weights and the network edges rendered in the visualization.

Part V

Limitations

Several gaps are worth naming honestly.

The candidate pool is only as good as the public lists and council prompts feeding it. Structurally influential actors who don't appear in major rankings and don't match council prompts may be systematically absent regardless of their actual impact. The ranking pipeline then converts LLM judgments about relative influence into ELO scores — judgments that reflect whatever priors the underlying models carry about what "influence" means. They are not grounded in behavioral observation or outcome data, and there is currently no formal evaluation of ranking drift or ranking validity between runs.

News coverage is mediated by Perplexity's retrieval and summarization, not raw web access. The briefings are therefore sensitive to what Perplexity surfaces in its 7-day window, which may underrepresent niche but consequential developments and overrepresent events that received broad media coverage.

Coactivation scores are LLM judgments about whether two actors' reported activities this week are reinforcing or adversarial within a given domain. They are observational proxies, not ground-truth measurements of actual cooperation or conflict. The decay mechanism prevents scores from becoming entrenched, but it also means that stable long-term relationships reset if they fall quiet for several consecutive weeks.

The structural vocabulary — the CA and PS canons — is fixed between explicit regeneration runs. As the real-world network of influence evolves, the model's conceptual scaffolding may drift out of alignment with it, and there is currently no automated mechanism for detecting or flagging that drift.

Finally, the pipeline is largely manually orchestrated. There is no continuous integration, no automated evaluation framework, and no alert when artifact quality degrades. The system is robust enough to produce weekly outputs, but it remains a research prototype rather than a production intelligence service. It is best understood as a structured way of paying attention — one that makes its priors and selection logic explicit, rather than hiding them inside an analyst's intuition.
