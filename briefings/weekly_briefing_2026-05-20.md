## Phase Sequence Pulse

**DPS-01: Executive Power Consolidation** [8/10] — *CA-027, CA-018, CA-030, CA-049, CA-006*  
The past week was marked by continued aggressive presidential consolidation of executive power, with the White House still issuing new presidential actions and executive orders while the administration pressed ahead on restructuring and tighter control of the federal bureaucracy. The biggest legal-development signal was the Supreme Court’s active engagement with a case that could significantly expand presidential authority over executive-branch officials, reinforcing that the administration’s push is now being tested at the highest level. At the same time, recent filings and opinions show growing judicial scrutiny of broad unilateral action, especially around tariffs and other delegated powers, suggesting the White House is advancing hard even as courts question the limits. Overall, the week reflected steady momentum toward a stronger unitary-executive model, with implementation, resistance, and litigation all moving in parallel.

**DPS-02: AI Dominance Race** [8/10] — *CA-065, CA-099, CA-055*  
Trump’s new “Framework for AI Diffusion,” which kicks in on May 15, 2025, is still reverberating through the ecosystem, with its hard cap that 50% of US-origin frontier compute must stay in the US and strict four‑tier export regime (zero advanced chips to China/Russia and tight quotas for India/Malaysia) driving hyperscalers like Nvidia and Oracle to accelerate overseas shipments and restructure where they assemble and host AI hardware. In parallel, US strategy has been formalized into “America’s AI Action Plan,” which codifies an overt “race” framing and ties export promotion, chip controls, and frontier‑model security evaluation into a single national AI‑industrial policy aimed explicitly at countering China. On the China side, open‑weight, aggressively priced models from labs such as DeepSeek, Z.AI, Moonshot, and others have now overtaken US models in global open‑source usage metrics, reinforcing Beijing’s “open AI” strategy as a tool of industrial and geopolitical influence. The UAE has emerged as a new strategic AI hub via G42’s “chip and data‑center diplomacy” and negotiations to host customized versions of US frontier models, signaling a shift toward a more multipolar AI infrastructure map rather than a pure US‑China duopoly.

**DPS-03: Judicial Pipeline & Doctrinal Shift** [7/10] — *CA-014, CA-102, CA-068*  
Federal courts continued to consolidate power over the administrative state this week, with lower courts invoking the Supreme Court’s recent anti‑deference and separation‑of‑powers precedents (West Virginia v. EPA, SEC v. Jarkesy, and the Chevron overruling) to narrow agency authority in environmental and financial‑regulation cases. Appellate panels increasingly framed disputes in “major questions” and Seventh Amendment terms, signaling a durable doctrinal shift that favors Article III adjudication and jury trials over agency tribunals for high‑stakes enforcement. Conservative legal networks and business‑aligned litigants leveraged these trends, filing and supporting test cases designed to extend Jarkesy‑style jury requirements and “clear congressional authorization” standards into new regulatory domains such as climate, tech, and financial oversight. Together, these moves indicate a maturing infrastructure in which strategic litigation, coordinated amicus briefs, and ideologically aligned judges are steadily re‑writing the balance of power between agencies, courts, and Congress.

**DPS-04: Fiscal-Monetary Stress Cycle** [5/10]  
The past week was relatively quiet on explicit policy moves but notable for expectations shifting toward a longer “higher-for-longer” plateau, reinforcing the structural tension between restrictive monetary policy and still‑elevated fiscal borrowing needs. Market commentary (e.g., Apollo, BofA/Merrill) emphasized continued economic resilience and fading expectations of additional 2026 rate cuts, which implies sustained real debt‑service pressure on the Treasury as large volumes of notes and bills roll at current yields. Futures and yield-curve pricing reflected this resilience by keeping near-term hike odds low but pushing out the timing of any easing cycle further, signaling that the Fed is prioritizing inflation control over fiscal accommodation. No major public clashes between the Fed and fiscal authorities surfaced, but the combination of strong macro data and delayed-cut narratives points to a slow-building stress cycle in which fiscal costs rise while the central bank remains in wait‑and‑see mode.

**DPS-05: Capital Allocation & Private Power** [8/10] — *CA-139, CA-058, CA-147*  
Private capital’s role in the energy transition continued to intensify, with new data underscoring both scale and direction of flows: private markets now manage on the order of $20–22 trillion in AUM, and an estimated $230 billion of that sits in thematic “energy transition” funds, three-quarters of which are dedicated clean‑energy vehicles (up from roughly 15% in 2015). Roughly $92 billion in those transition funds remains un-deployed “dry powder,” signaling imminent acceleration of ownership shifts into real assets like wind, solar, grids, and storage, where private funds already dominate infrastructure-style investments. Market commentary from law and advisory firms points to a rebound in U.S. private equity deal value—despite softer deal counts—suggesting large sponsors are prioritizing fewer, bigger, higher‑influence bets, especially in energy and infrastructure adjacencies. Parallel ESG and capital-allocation guidance from major consultancies is pushing corporates—particularly in resource and energy sectors—to redesign capital frameworks around decarbonization and “purpose,” effectively aligning listed companies’ strategies with the portfolio interests and policy narratives favored by large alternative asset managers.

**DPS-06: Big Tech Regulatory Arbitrage** [4/10]  
Regulatory arbitrage by Big Tech saw limited but telling movement over the past week, mostly around AI and digital assets rather than classic social-media antitrust fights. Internationally, the UNU piece on “AI governance arbitrage” is gaining traction in policy circles, reinforcing a narrative that firms are exploiting gaps between national and international AI rules and pushing governments toward mutual recognition of safety standards and transparency requirements as conditions for market access—this sets the stage for closing some cross‑border arbitrage but remains aspirational. In the U.S., continued delay of the CLARITY Act sustains a fragmented crypto/digital‑asset regime, giving large platforms and fintechs room to structure products across SEC/CFTC/FinCEN seams, even as the article signals that once enacted, tougher AML, traceability, and operational‑readiness expectations could sharply narrow that space. Regional and community banks’ 2026 tech‑risk guidance hints at rising regulatory scrutiny of cloud dependence and agentic AI, which may indirectly constrain the ability of hyperscale cloud and AI providers to use contractual and technical lock‑in as a regulatory end‑run, but these are still early‑stage signals rather than concrete enforcement actions.

**DPS-07: Defense-Industrial Expansion** [6/10]  
The biggest signal this week is continued federal push to modernize the defense-industrial base around speed, resilience, and allied interoperability, building on the Pentagon’s newer strategy framework that emphasizes supply-chain hardening, workforce readiness, flexible acquisition, and economic deterrence. The structural direction is toward more public-private risk-sharing, larger stockpiles of critical systems, supplier diversification, and faster pathways for integrating commercial technology into defense production and sustainment. The policy backdrop also remains strongly pro-expansion, with recent White House emphasis on overhauling acquisition processes to improve execution and innovation. Overall, the week looks more like sustained momentum than a single breakout event, but the trend line is clearly toward deeper defense-industrial mobilization.

**DPS-08: Information Environment Contestation** [6/10]  
Major developments this week centered on U.S. federal policy moves that structurally reshape the information environment around environmental and AI-related issues. The new EDGI report documents extensive deletion of environmental justice, climate, and “environmental racism” content from federal websites in the early months of Trump’s second term—an aggressive attempt to narrow the informational baseline available to media, activists, and the public. In parallel, Executive Order 14318 and the associated White House AI Action Plan push agencies to streamline environmental review for data centers and related infrastructure, signaling closer alignment between federal information flows and the priorities of AI, energy, and semiconductor industries. Together, these moves tilt narrative and regulatory terrain toward growth-first, industry-friendly framing while eroding official online resources that historically supported environmental and justice-focused counter-narratives.

**DPS-09: Energy Transition & Security** [7/10]  
DOE advanced the energy–compute agenda this week by announcing new funding for grid upgrades and long-duration storage pilots aimed at supporting data center and AI-driven load growth, while also signaling forthcoming guidance on how IRA tax credits can be stacked for firm clean power projects. FERC inched forward on transmission reform, with staff indicating that forthcoming rules will prioritize multi-state high‑voltage lines and interconnection queue reforms, reinforcing a structural tilt toward large-scale renewables and storage over new standalone gas capacity. In the private sector, several major utilities and hyperscale cloud providers disclosed new power purchase and build‑transfer agreements for nuclear-adjacent small modular reactor projects and large solar-plus-battery installations, underscoring a shift from short-term RECs toward long-lived, dispatchable or firmed clean assets. Meanwhile, gas and pipeline interests intensified lobbying around “reliability” in ongoing EPA and congressional debates, but no decisive federal rollback materialized, suggesting incumbents are now fighting to shape rather than stop the transition.

**DPS-10: Coalition Machinery & Electoral Infrastructure** [6/10]  
Republican-aligned super PACs and dark-money groups continued consolidating 2026 Senate and House battlefield investments, with several reports of early reservations for fall ad buys in key Midwestern and Sun Belt states, signaling a shift from candidate- to party-centric spending control. On the Democratic side, party committees coordinated with major progressive donor networks to shore up funding for vulnerable incumbents and to scale new voter-targeting programs centered on abortion and democracy-protection messaging, reflecting a discipline push after uneven primaries. Several state-level legal fights over ballot access and new voter-ID or signature-matching rules advanced, with courts in at least two battleground states allowing restrictive provisions to remain in effect for now, forcing campaigns to retool field and legal strategies. Finally, bipartisan establishment “pro-democracy” coalitions, including business-backed groups, quietly expanded funding for election-administration defense and poll-worker recruitment in swing counties, indicating sustained elite concern over election subversion risks.
